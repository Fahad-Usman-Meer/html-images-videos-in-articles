This is a compilation of musical terms in music reviews, printed scores, and program notes. In compliance with the Italian roots of several European musical norms, most of the words are Italian. The distinctive musical definitions of these phrases are sometimes different from the original or existing Italian interpretations. Many of several other terms are noted by "Fr." and "Ger." Indicating their French and German origin.

The words are Italian or English unless otherwise indicated. Some words are prevalent, and others are only infrequently used, and periodically new words are created. Instead of the classic terms referenced here, some composers prefer words from their own language.

To reinforce your music understanding, here is a list of musical terms:

a or à (Fr.): at, for, to, by, in

à la (Fr.): in style like…

a Battuta: Following a deviation, return to normal tempo. Not recommended because of possible confusion with battuto (qv.) in string parts; use a tempo, which amounts to the same thing.

a bene placito: Up to the artist

a cappella: Only vocal components, without instrumental music

a capriccio: A free and unpredictable approach to tempo

a due (a 2): Meant as a duet; together; for two voices or instruments; Following a solo passage for one of the instruments, two instruments must be played in unison.

a niente: Indicating a diminuendo which fades completely away; to nothing.

a piacere: At pleasure. For instance, it is not necessary for the performer to strictly follow the rhythm.

a prima vista: lit. "at first sight". Sight-reading. For instance, played or sung from presented notation without the written material being reviewed previously; refer to the figure.

a tempo: In time. For instance, the performer must go back to the original tempo of the work, such as after a ritardando or accelerando; It can also be found in association with other specific instances, such as giusto tempo (in strict time) or a tempo di menuetto (at the speed of a minuet)

Ab (Ger.): organ stops or mutes, off.

Abafando (Port.): muted, muffled.

Abandon or Avec (Fr.): unrestrained, free, passionate.

Abbandonatamente, con abbandono: in relaxed mode, freely,

Aber (Ger.): but.

Accarezzevole: Caressing and expressive.

Accelerando (accel.): Gradually increasing the tempo; accelerating.

Accelerato: with heightened tempo

Accent: Emphasis; accent.

accentato/accentuato: With emphasis; accented.

Acceso: On fire; ignited.

Accessible: Music that is easy to understand or listen to.

Acciaccato: Crushed; broken down, the sounding of the notes of a chord not exactly simultaneously, but from bottom to top.

Acciaccatura: Crushing. For instance, a fast grace note that is "crushed" against the note that follows and takes up no value in the measure.

Accompagnato: Accompanied. For instance, with the accompaniment following the soloist, which can accelerate or slow down at will.

Accuratezza: Accuracy; con accuratezza: with precision; precision.

Acoustic: Concerning music generated by instruments, contrasted with electrical or electronic instruments

ad libitum (usually ad-lib; Latin): At liberty. For instance, the speed and method of performance are left to the performer. It may mean improvisation as well.

Adagietto: Slowly but faster than adagio

Adagio: Slowly

Adagissimo: Very slowly; very.

Affannoso, affannato: Anguished.

Con affetto or affetto: with affect or emotion.

Affettuosamente, affettuoso, or affectueusement (Fr.): With emotion or affect; see also con affetto.

Affrettando: Pressing onwards, Hurrying.

Agile: Nimble; Agile.

Agitato: Agitated

al or alla: In the manner of (al before masculine nouns, alla before feminine); to the.

Alcuna licenza: Used in con alcuna licenza, denoting ‘play’ with some freedom in the moment, see rubato.

Alla breve: Two beats per measure or the equivalent thereof; in cut-time.

Alla marcia: In the fashion of a march

Alla polacca: In the fashion of a polonaise.

Allargando: Becoming progressively slower; Broadening.

Allegretto: Moderately fast; a little lively,

Allegretto vivace: A moderately fast tempo

Allegrezza: Joyfulness; Cheerfulness.

Allegrissimo: Quite fast, although slower than presto

Allegro: Cheerful or brisk; generally interpreted as fast, lively.

All'ottava: see ottava, "at the octave".

Alt (Eng.) altered dominant or alt dom: A jazz descriptor that directs musicians, such as a jazz guitarist or a jazz pianist, to play a dominant (V7) chord with at least one modified (flattened or sharpened) 5th or 9th chord.

Altissimo: see also in altissimo. Very high.

Alto: It frequently refers to a specific vocal range. High; higher than a tenor, but lower than a soprano

Alzate sordini: For instance, remove mutes. Lift or raise the mutes.

Am Steg (Ger.): See sul ponticello. At the bridge. For instance, playing near the bridge of a bowed string instrument, which creates a heavier, stronger tone.

Amabile: Pleasant; Amiable.

Ambitus: Range of notes between the highest and lowest

Amore or amor (Sp./Port., sometimes It.): con amore: with love, tenderly; Love.

Amoroso: Loving

Anacrusis: A pickup; A note or notes preceding the first full bar.

Andamento: Often used concerning a fugue subject of higher-than-average length

Andante: At a walking pace. For instance, at a modest tempo.

Andantino: It was, sometimes used previously to mean a little slower than andante. A little bit faster than the andante.

ängstlich (Ger.): Anxiously

Anima: con anima: with feeling; Soul.

Animandosi: Increasingly more animated

Animato: Lively; animated.

Antiphon: A liturgical or composition comprised of choral responses, often between two choirs; a passage making it part of another composition of this nature; a repetitive passage, like a refrain, in a psalm or any other liturgical piece.

Antiphonal: A composition style in which two sections of performers or instrumentalists swap sections or music one after the other; usually, the performers are on opposite ends of a hall or venue.

Apaisé (Fr.): Calmed

Appassionato: Fervent; Passionate.

Leaning note or appoggiatura: One or several grace notes that occupy the note value of the succeeding full note.

Arco: A bow used to play some string instruments. Normally used to cancel a pizzicato direction. For instance, played with the bow, as opposed to pizzicato, in music for bowed instruments.

Aria: Normally with orchestral accompaniment, a self-contained part for one voice. This may be presented using an orchestral reduction by a pianist.

Arietta: A short aria

Arioso: Like an air, a melody. For instance, in the manner of an aria, melodious; Airy.

Armonioso: Harmonious

Arpeggiato, arpeggio: Played like a harp. In piano music, this is often a solution for playing a broad chord whose notes cannot be performed otherwise; arpeggios are often used as a melody; see also broken chords. The notes of the chords should be played swiftly, one after another rather than sequentially.

Articulato: Articulate

Assai: Very much; Much.

Assez (Fr.): Sufficiently; Enough.

Attacca: Attach or attack; go straight on. A direction for attaching the next movement to the preceding one, at the end of a movement, without a gap or pause. Also used as "attacca subito," implying a transition from "sudden" movement. "Attack suddenly".

Ausdruck (Ger.): Expression.

Ausdrucksvoll or mit Ausdruck (Ger.): With expression; Expressively.

Avec (Fr.): With.

Ballad: From the vulgar Latin 'ballare,' which means dance. A work with a narrative structure that imitates a folk song in a dance form.

Bar: A vertical line through the stave that marks the music into sections with a certain number of beats inside.

Barcarolle: A boating song that usually describes the songs sung in Venice by gondoliers.

Baritone: Male voice singing in the middle of the range.

Baroque: The era between 1600 and 1750 of art and music.

Basso Continuo: A type of bass line that was used in the Baroque period. Typically, it is notated with numbers denoting what chords can be utilised so the lines can be embellished by the continuo player.

Berceuse: A lullaby. Normally slow and undulating.

Bolero: A Spanish dance.

Cadence: At the end of a piece, two chords that offer a kind of 'punctuation' at the final moment of a musical sentence. Cadences can either indicate that the sentence is not over or offer a type of musical 'full-stop'.

Cantata: A choral work with an instrumental (usually orchestral) melody that utilises solo voices. In a broad sense, a cantata is a choral work of some length that utilises solo voices, typically with instrumental accompaniment. Some cantatas, without a chorus or choir, use solo voices.

Capriccio: A vibrant piece of music, generally free and short in its form.

Chanson (Fr.): From medieval to 20th century, a French song.

Chorale: A Lutheran hymn. Commonly, the music moves in block chords.

Chord: The playing of two or more notes simultaneously.

Chromatic: Notes that do not form part of the diatonic scale. For instance, in the scale of C major (white notes on the piano), the black keys (sharps and flats) are the chromatic notes.

Clef: Several symbols at the end of a stave show the notes' pitch drawn up on that stave.

Coda: The tail end of a music piece. Typically, a portion that shows the end of the part or section approaches.

Coloratura: A form of decoration that is typically ornate and richly ornamented in singing.

Concerto: A part of instrumental music for soloist contrasted by way of an ensemble. Be it a small group or a complete orchestra of musicians.

Countertenor: The vocal range of the male alto. Close to a female soprano.

Crescendo: A dynamic instruction that means playing louder progressively.

Da Capo: Normally abbreviated to 'D.C.' at the final moment of a section of a piece, which means to go back to start and play at either the end (Da capo al fine) or to the sign that looks like an "S" (Da capo al segno).

Diminuendo: Literally 'diminishing'. A dynamic instruction which means to progressively play quieter.

Dynamics: The Sound Levels in Music. A spectrum between soft and loud.

Elegy: A musical piece in the style of a lament.

Ensemble: Instrumentalists playing together; a group of performers.

Espressivo: Expressive. An instruction to play a passage expressively or with expression.

Etude (Fr.): An instrumental composition aimed at improving or taxing certain technical aspects.

Fagotto: Italian expression for Bassoon.

Flat: Illustrated by a stylised ♭ symbol. It indicates that a note before which it is positioned should be lowered by a semitone. Flat can also imply that a note is out of tune.

Forte: 'Strong'. A detailed instruction that means that the music should be played loudly. The statement appears either as: 'f' loud; 'ff' fortissimo, which means very loud; or 'fff' very loud. It largely depends on how loud a composer intends for something to be played. The practice has developed to enable any amount of 'f's.

1 3⁄5′: Tierce organ stop.

2 2⁄3′: A pipe organ stop for the 12th interval.

1′: "sifflet" or one-foot organ stop.

16′: sixteen-foot pipe, pipe organ signal calling for one octave below 8′

2′: two feet, pipe organ signal

32′: thirty-two-foot pipe, pipe organ signal calling for two octaves below 8′, also known as sub-bass.

4′: four feet, pipe organ rank that speaks one octave higher than 8′

64′: sixty-four-foot pipe, pipe organ signal

8′: eight-foot pipe – pipe organ signal

I: usually for orchestral string instruments, applied to indicate that the player should play the passage on the highest-pitched, thinnest string.

II: cymbal stop on the pipe organ

II: typically for orchestral string instruments, applied to signify that the player should play the passage on the string adjacent to the maximum-pitched, thinnest string, for instance, the 2nd highest string

III: typically for orchestral string instruments, applied to signify that the player should play the string passage adjacent to (but higher in pitch) than the lowest-pitched, thickest string, for instance, the 3rd highest string

IV: typically for orchestral string instruments, used to signify that the player should play the passage on the lowest-pitched, thickest string, for instance, the 4th highest string

IV-VI: mixture stop on the pipe organ.
